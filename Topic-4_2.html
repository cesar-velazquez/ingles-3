Script

Interviewer: So, what makes you feel nervous?

Katty: Let´s see. Well, I really hate the idea of speaking in public.

Interviewer: Have you ever talked to a room full of people?

Katty: No, I haven´t. In my job, I´m a TV reporter, I talk to a lot of people, but one at a time, not all together. No, I´ve never talked to a room full of people – and I never want to!

Interviewer: And what about interviewing people? Does that make you feel nervous?

Katty: No, not at all. Well, it depends on the people, I suppose. Some interviews can be difficult.

Interviewer: Have you ever met any famous people? Have you ever interviewed a film star or, I don´t know, a President?

Katty: Yes, I have. I´ve interviewed famous people. I interviewed Robert de Niro once.

Interviewer: Did you feel nervous?

Katty: No, not at all. He was really easy to talk to.

Interviewer: So, is there anything else that makes you feel nervous?

Katty: Yes, I don´t really like the idea of flying. Especially in small airplanes.

Interviewer: Have you ever flown in a small airplane?

Katty: Yes, I have. Over the Amazon. We were making a programme about the Amazon people. I was absolutely terrified! I never want to do that again.

We use present perfect to talk about our experiences for our whole life or our recent experiences.

This chart shows the affirmative, negative and interrogative form of the PRESENT PERFECT tense.

Interrogative form

Subject + HAVE / HAS + Past participle

Past participle for regular Verbs: -ed

  • Robert has worked hard all his life.
    • work > worked
    • play > played
    • clean> cleaned
    • start > started
    • dance > danced

Past participle for irregular verbs

  • I have been in Canada three times.
  • She has had a lot of troubles recently.
  • They have never seen their father.

TO REMEMBER:

Contractions:

  • I have been = I´ve been
  • She has lived= She´s lived

Subject + HAVE / HAS + NOT + Past participle

  • I haven’t seen you for ages!
  • Scientists haven’t found a cure for AIDS.
  • It hasn’t rained since August.

TO REMEMBER:

We can make negative sentences by using "not" or "never":

  • I have not seen her today.
  • I have never seen her. (0 times in my life)

HAVE / HAS + subject + Past participle?

  • Have the children finished their homework?
  • Has Susan gone to bed?
  • Where have you been?

Short answers:

  • Have you finished your work? Yes, I have.
  • Has Tom eaten his dinner? No, he hasn´t.

TO REMEMBER:

We use questions with Have you ever + verb ...? to ask about experiences:

Have you ever been to London?

The use of ever intensifies a question, but it’s perfectly fine to ask questions without ever.

The present perfect tense is often used with adverbial expressions or prepositions such as:

already, yet, just, ever, never since, for.

These are the uses of the PRESENT PERFECT tense.

Action started in the past which continues up to the present:

  • It has rained for about 3 days. (It continues raining.)
  • I have lived in Bristol since 1984. (I continue living there.)

Action in the past with a result in the present:

  • I´ve lost my keys. (so I can´t get into my house.)

Talk about experiences:

  • Have you ever seen an igloo?
  • I have never experienced a hurricane.

Script

Interviewer: So, what makes you feel nervous?

Mark: Mmm, let´s see. I´ve always hated spiders. They make me feel really nervous.

Interviewer: Have you ever held a spider in your hand? 

Mark: No! Never! I don´t think I could. 

Interviewer: Why don´t you like spiders?

Mark: I don´t know. I just don´t.

Interviewer: Is there anything else you don´t like?

Mark: Well, let´s see. Yes, I really don´t like heights. 

Interviewer: Have you ever stood on top of a tall building? 

Mark: Yes, I have. And I was terrified. I couldn´t look down. I just stood really close to the wall and closed my eyes. It was awful.

Interviewer: Anything else that make you feel nervous? 

Mark: Well, yes there is! You ´re  going to think I´m  a really nervous person, but I hate fast cars. They make me feel really bad.

Interviewer: Do you drive?

Mark: Yes, I do. 

Interviewer: And have you ever driven a fast car? I mean, a sports car? 

Mark: No, I haven´t. One of my friends took me out in a Porsche once, but I´ve never  driven a fast car myself. May be it´s the driver that makes me nervous, and not the  car!

Recap of Topic 4.2 Talking about life experiences

Listen to the audio.

You learned how to use the present perfect tense to talk about life experiences.

Remember that we need the verb have as auxiliary verb and the main verb goes in past participle to form the present perfect tense in English.

Examples:

  • I have travelled abroad twice in my life.
  • My mother hasn´t been in China / My mother has never been in China.
  • Has she finished her homework? Yes, she has. / No, she hasn´t.
