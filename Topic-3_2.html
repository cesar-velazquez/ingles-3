When & While

Before you do some practice, it is important to identify when to use these two words.

  • While is usually used with past continuous:

My mother was cooking dinner while I was watching a movie. (Simultaneous actions in the past).

While I was waiting for the bus, it started to rain. (Action in progress in the past when interrupted by another action).

  • When is used before past simple:

Mary was taking a shower when her friend called.

  • When is usually used with past simple:

When she saw the cat, she yelled at it… (An action that happened in the course of another action).

Who were they talking to when you saw them? (A continuous long action is interrupted by a short action).

Notice that when you use While or When at the beginning of the sentence, a comma is used.

The Wolf and the Donkey

A Donkey was eating happily near the woods when he saw a Wolf. The Wolf was trying to get closer so the Donkey soon realized what the Wolf was thinking about. So, he thought of a plan to save himself. He started to walk like if he were hurt.

When the Wolf finally came up to him, he asked the Donkey why he was walking with difficulty. The Donkey replied that he had a sharp thorn in his back leg. “Please Mr. Wolf, take it out!” He asked. “If not, the thorn could stick in your throat when you eat me.” The Wolf thought it was a good suggestion and asked the Donkey to lift up his foot. He began to search carefully. While he was looking at his foot, the Donkey asked him to look closer.

Just then, the Donkey kicked out as hard as he could sending the Wolf some feet away. And while the Wolf was getting very slowly and painfully to his feet, the Donkey ran as far as he could to be safe.

“Serves me right,” thought the Wolf as he was trying to get on his feet. “I’m a butcher by trade, not a doctor!”
