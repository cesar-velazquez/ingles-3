As you can see, there are certain prepositions and adverbs which are used in the present perfect tense.

Now, look at the following information:

Uses

Ever indicates “at any time in your life until now.”

Structure

Ever

  • It is used in questions about life experiences:
    • Have you ever travelled abroad?
    • Has she ever been in London?
  • Do not use ever with already.

Uses

Never indicates “at no time in your life.”

Structure

Never

I’ve never had a dog.

Uses

Yet= until now. Yet shows that the speaker is expecting something to happen.

Structure

Yet

  • Use yet only in questions and negative sentences; it usually goes at the end of the sentence:
    • Has it stopped raining yet?
    • I’ve written the email, but I haven’t sent it yet.

Uses

Just = a short time ago.

Structure

Just

  • We can use the present perfect with just:
    • Are you hungry? No, I’ve just had lunch.
    • Hello. Have you just arrived?

Uses

We use already to express recent experiences or that something happened sooner than expected.

Structure

Already

  • Use already also in questions and in affirmative statements:
    • Don’t forget to to pay your electicity bill. I’ve already paid it.
    • What time is Mark leaving? He’s already left.
    • Has she already tried Korean barbecue? I have already tried sushi.
  • Do not use already in negative sentences.

Uses

How long...? (+ present perfect)

Structure

How long

  • Use how long... to make present perfect questions.
  • The answer to this question might include for or since depending on the type of information we are looking for.

Uses

For is used to refer to a period of time in which an action has happened.

Structure

FOR + a period of time.

Uses

Since is used to refer to an exact time in the past when an action started and continues (a past time clause).

Structure

SINCE + a past time clause.

  • How long have they been married? They have been married for 15 years.
  • How long has he been in hospital? He has been in hospital since Monday.
  • How long have you worked here? I’ve worked here for a long time.
  • How long have they known each other? They’ve known each other since they were at school.

Recap of Topic 4.3 How long?

Listen to the audio.

So far you have learned that the present perfect tense can be used with some adverbs and prepositions such as ever, never, yet, how long, just, already, for and since to talk about an unidentified time, to talk about an action that happened before in a nonspecific time, to indicate how much an action might last or the moment in which an action started and continues:

  • Have you ever tried mushrooms?
  • I have never eaten Irish food.
  • They haven’t arrived yet.
  • How long have you ridden a bike?
  • She has already bought that book, don’t buy it.
  • We have been together for more than 5 years.
  • We have won the race many times, since 2019.
